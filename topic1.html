Does Iran intend to make a nuclear bomb?

If they wanted to, they would have

It’s been widely accepted that Iran has had the capability to make the Nuclear bomb for last decade. The reason that they have not till now is that they understand that the global backlash from possessing nuclear weaponry without being officially recognized as a nuclear state would outweigh the defensive benefits of owning the weapon. Iran ia aware that the likeliness of a nuclear bomb being used in today’s world is next to none. Therefore the country’s politicians believe the simply symbolic show of strength is not worth invoking subsequent global and unanimous opposition.

A more advanced uranium enrichment system is needed to make sufficient enriched uranium for nuclear power than a nuclear bomb. Iran has been operating a nuclear power plant for 4 years.

Unstable politics, unstable promises

While Iran’s current president, Hassan Rouhani, has often dismissed his country’s nuclear weaponry intentions, hardliners within Iran’s political hierarchy have often called for the weapons to be made. With the unstable political climate in Iran as well as the surrounding middle eastern countries, Rouhani is not guaranteed control of the country and so its nuclear programme. There have been several rumours of imminent insurgencies in the country so Rouhani’s promise to not make the bomb, while trustable, might well be irrelevant soon.

Rouhani’s approval ratings have been falling continuously for the past 9 months.

Deal hinders Iran’s path to the bomb

At the current rate at which Iran is already developing its nuclear program, this deal can only hinder their progress. In truth, Iran have had the resources and intelligence to make a nuclear weapon for the past eight years. This deal forces Iran to cap its centrifuges at very low levels, keep enrichment levels well below those that could be used for weapons and convert its existing enriched uranium into fuel rods (which could not be put to military use). Iran is looking to be accepted as a nuclear state and are willing to sacrifice the development of their facilities for global acceptance. They are not using this deal to fast-track their path to nuclear weaponry.

The proposed deal is rumoured to disallow Iran from developing nuclear weaponry for at least the next 9 years.

What are Rouhani's intentions behind the nuclear proposal?

Rouhani does not want a nuclear bomb

Hassan Rouhani was elected in 2013 in hopes of de-escalating tensions with the West by reaching a comprehensive nuclear deal that would remove international sanctions against Iran. He claims that atomic weapons have not kept nuclear states safe and as such, Iran is not seeking the bomb. Rouhani did not only run as a moderate but headed a delegation that formulated a comprehensive plan to de-escalate tensions with the West and increase confidence in Iran -- in the hope of bringing nuclear energy and not weapons to Iran.

Iran is a signatory to the NPT and says it will remain committed to its obligations not to build nuclear weapons under the treaty but will not compromise on its right to enrich uranium and produce nuclear fuel.

IAEA official questions Rouhani

Skepticism persists over whether Iran is in it for the nuclear weapons. IAEA official Heinonen, who has a long record of dealing with Iranian leaders, claims that Rouhani boasted of how he used talks with the West to “buy time to advance Iran’s programme.” As negotiations draw longer, it seems that the deal is not being implemented in good faith and that Iran is playing games.

IAEA 2004 landmark report disclosed that Iran had failed to report that it had imported uranium hexafluoride, which produces fuel for nuclear weapons.

Rouhani’s political career on the edge

Iran’s foreign minister, Zarif, warned that failure to agree a nuclear deal would herald the political demise of President Rouhani. Although Zarif denies these claims, a failed deal would supposedly empower Iran’s conservative hardliners, who are part of the Revolutionary Guard Corps, and decrease the chances of Iran’s nuclear program being curbed. In fact, the country is split in their opinion of the nuclear weapons program. As a result, Rouhani is pushing this deal forward.

In November, hardline students took to the streets in November to denounce moderate Rouhani and demand their government not cave to Western pressures and hold onto a full-scale nuclear program.

What does a nuclear Iran mean to regional political stability?

Hezbollah links

Iran and its government’s recurring links to Hezbollah serve as a red flag for nuclear help to ever be provided to Iran. Just last week, an Israeli investigation on an infiltrated Hezbollah base revealed that army generals had been sharing intelligence with the Hezbollah hierarchy. This coincides with Iran’s increasing state of economic turmoil since the plummet of oil prices. Being able to sell nuclear weaponry will certainly offset that. Giving nuclear resources such as enriched uranium and weapons grade plutonium to Iran is akin to giving nuclear weaponry to Hezbollah. Iran’s access to such materials raises the threat level.

In order for Iran to balance their budget they need oil to be priced over $136 per barrel. Oil is today being sold at $50 a barrel.

The Dangers of a Failed Nuclear Deal

Iran has come to the table with proposed nuclear deals several times in past decades. Past rejections were motivated by the thought that Iran could not progress as a non-recognised nuclear state. This was repeatedly proved wrong. Iran’s nuclear program has domestically been one of their few points of pride and a further rejection will simply lead to further unconstrained development. However, today they are capable of developing a nuclear weapon. This would fuel Iran’s very public conflict with Israel.

Since the last time a nuclear deal with Iran has been rejected, the number of nuclear centrifuges owned by Iran has increased from 50 to 19,000.

Nuclear Balance, Stable Region

Israel's regional nuclear monopoly, has long fueled instability in the Middle East. In no other region of the world does a lone, unchecked nuclear state exist. It is Israel's supposed nuclear arsenal, not Iran's desire for one, that has contributed most to the current crisis. By reducing nuclear imbalances in military power, a nuclear Iran can catalyse more regional and international stability.

What ceased the escalation of the Cold War was the fear of nuclear retaliation if a nuclear bomb was dropped.

Will a nuclear deal for Iran go through?

Obama Ready to Walk Away

President Obama claims that the US has offered Iran an “extraordinarily reasonable” nuclear deal in an effort to move negotiations in the right direction. The central contention is getting Iran to narrow the gaps in the deal that would allow Iran to make a nuclear weapon. If the deal does not ensure this, then the US will not accept it. This means the deal must provide intrusive inspections and close Iran’s nuclear pathways. Two years of negotiations indicate Iran is not ready to accept this kind of deal.

Negotiations over this deal have lasted 2 years and the deadline has been extended twice.

Netanyahu Getting in the Way

In his speech, Netanyahu warns Congress that a proposed agreement between world powers and Iran would categorically not stop Tehran from getting nuclear weapons -- leaving Israel in great peril. Netanyahu appealed to Obama’s Republican critics in saying that the proposed agreement was a bad deal and that negotiations must keep going -- sparking deafening cheers from the House of Representatives. The speech was an open critique of the White House, darkening relations between Israel and Washington. If Israel’s rhetoric continues to split Congress, a deal is improbable.

Netanyahu’s speech was interrupted by multiple standing ovations by Republicans.

Israel’s “scaremongering” won’t stop deal

Iran’s top nuclear negotiator said that “scaremongering” by Netanyahu will not stop a nuclear deal from passing. He claims that Israel is trying to incite fear within other countries to prevent peace but that leaders will be able to see through these dirty tactics. With an early interim accord reached in 2013, there is a common desire to pass a deal and achieve peace and even Israel cannot get in the way of that.

An interim accord between US, Russia, China, Britain, France and Germany was reached in November 2013.

Rouhani’s referendum to pass the deal

Rouhani realizes that sanctions have put Iran in a difficult economic situation. He claims “our ideals are not bound to centrifuges” and appeals to the country’s hardliners to reconsider the scope of their uranium enrichment program. Rouhani notes that under the constitution, Iranians are entitled to have important issues put to a referendum instead of a parliamentary issue. This would make the deal difficult to oppose by hardliners. This suggests that Rouhani’s intentions are to pass the deal.

In 2004 Rouhani, while heading the Supreme National Security Council and leading Iran's negotiations with Western powers regarding the nuclear deal, he suggested putting the nuclear programme to public vote.

What are Netanyahu’s reasons for pleading to Congress?

Fear

Netanyahu is applying his usual scaremongering tactics to a US congress he knows is heavily against the Obama administration. He is trying to put pressure on Obama by appealing to the Republicans. Something he managed to do successfully judging by the unnecessary amount of clapping exhibited.

Israel’s ambassador to Switzerland was fired following this tweet: "Is it no longer possible to suffice in scaring us here in Hebrew? [Netanyahu] has to fly all the way to the US Congress and tell them in English how dangerous Iran's nuclear program is?"

Showing Strength

Netanyahu used this opportunity to embarrass the President hoping it would undermine Obama’s efforts to reach a deal with Iran. By being invited by a speaker of the congress as opposed to the president and then riddling his speech with digs at Obama, Netanyahu wanted to show his supporters and opposition at home that he was not being silenced by the United States.

This is the first time that a foreign leader, who was not invited by the white house, has come to speak in front of congress.

Warning the world

The reason for speaking in congress was in fact not an opportunity for Netanyahu to publically insult the US president. Instead, he wanted to warn the world of what he truly believes is a grave mistake being made by allowing Iran to possess nuclear capabilities. He is adamant that Iran’s nuclear ambitions are malicious. With all eyes on him for once, Netanyahu wanted to bring up points he believed would persuade world leaders to have a second look at this deal.

A recent IAEA report on Iran revealed that Iran had lied to the world about the structuring of its nuclear facilities and the way it had obtained uranium.
